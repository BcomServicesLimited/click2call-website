# Handling Rules by Email Type

This file provides practical handling rules for common inbox scenarios.

## General handling standard

Unless a sender-specific exception is approved, inbound emails should be handled under the approval-only workflow. The usual path is to review the email, classify the intent, prepare a draft if appropriate, and mark the outcome as ready for approval, needs clarification, or needs escalation.

## 1. New sales enquiry

When the email is a general sales enquiry, prepare a helpful draft reply that briefly explains the relevant Click2Call service category and asks the minimum qualifying questions needed to guide the customer.

| Ask about | Reason |
|---|---|
| Number of users | Helps identify likely service scale |
| Need for new numbers or existing number retention | Determines whether number setup or porting is relevant |
| Devices to be used | Helps distinguish desk phones, softphones, and Teams use cases |
| Required features | Identifies business hours, voicemail, call routing, call recording, or AI receptionist needs |

If the customer asks for custom pricing, discounts, or non-standard arrangements, draft only a short acknowledgement and escalate.

## 2. Pricing enquiry

When the email asks about standard pricing, prepare a draft using published pricing only. Make it clear that the figures are based on current published website pricing and may depend on the final service configuration.

If the customer asks for a tailored quote, discounted pricing, wholesale terms, bundle pricing, or any commitment outside published information, escalate.

## 3. Support request

When the email is a routine support enquiry, prepare a draft that acknowledges the issue and asks only for the information needed to diagnose the problem.

| Request detail | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Account email address | Helps identify the account |
| Affected number or service | Helps narrow the issue |
| What is happening | Establishes the actual fault or request |
| Error message or screenshot | Improves diagnostic accuracy |
| When the issue started | Helps determine urgency and scope |

If the message suggests a service outage, a widespread fault, or an issue affecting multiple users or numbers, escalate.

## 4. Number porting enquiry

Number porting requests should be treated as escalation matters. A draft may acknowledge the request and ask for the minimum identifying information, but the matter should still be marked for escalation before any substantive process guidance is approved.

## 5. Billing or refund enquiry

Billing disputes, payment issues, refund requests, and requests for credits should always be escalated. Prepare only a polite acknowledgement draft and avoid making financial commitments.

## 6. Complaint or legal concern

If the email includes a complaint, threat, legal issue, compliance concern, or reputational risk, do not attempt a full resolution draft. Prepare only a calm acknowledgement and escalate immediately.

## 7. Suspicious or security-sensitive email

If an email appears suspicious, asks for account access changes, requests private account data, or contains phishing indicators, do not provide sensitive information. Mark it for escalation.

## 8. Approved sender exception

Emails from `royce.clark@gmail.com` are an approved exception. They may be responded to automatically without separate approval before sending, provided the reply still follows the tone, accuracy, and escalation rules in the main policy file.
